A PSA 9 on a Moonbreon sells for roughly $300 raw; a PSA 10 clears $900 or more. The difference is almost always visible before you put it in a submission envelope, if you know what to look for.

This post gives you a working pre-grade checklist built around the same four criteria PSA graders use: centering, surfaces, edges, and corners. It's a companion to the [Pokémon card condition guide](/pokemon-card-condition-guide), which covers the broader grading spectrum. Here, the focus is narrow: filter out the cards that will come back PSA 8 or lower before you spend a single dollar on submission fees.

## Why Pre-Grading Matters More in 2026

PSA's standard service fee sits at $25 per card as of 2026, and that's before shipping, insurance, and the 2-3 month turnaround. Submit 20 cards, get 15 PSA 8s back, and you've burned $375 on cards that may not have moved the needle on resale value at all. The PSA 8 premium over raw for most modern cards is thin, often under $15 per card once fees are factored in.

The math gets worse if you're submitting high-value targets. At $50 or $100 per card on express tiers, a misread PSA 8 on a card you expected to be a 10 is a real loss. Pre-grading is just loss prevention.

Foil's scan data across cards processed confirms that misidentification at the variant level, reading the wrong set code or collector number off a card, is one of the most consistent upstream errors in a submission pipeline. If you're pricing comps against the wrong variant, your entire grade decision is built on bad data.

<Callout variant="tip">The four PSA criteria are graded holistically, but PSA's published standards indicate centering is evaluated first because it's objective and measurable. A card that fails centering cannot be a PSA 10, regardless of surface quality.</Callout>

## Centering: The 55/45 Rule and How to Measure It

Centering is the most actionable criterion because you can measure it with a ruler. PSA 10 requires the front to fall within roughly 55/45 left-to-right and top-to-bottom; PSA 9 allows approximately 60/40. CenterGrade's published breakdown confirms these tolerances as the industry reference point for modern Pokémon cards.

How to measure it yourself: use a digital caliper or a fine ruler. Measure the white border on the left side, then the right. Measure top, then bottom. If your left border is 2.1mm and your right is 3.4mm, that's approximately 62/38, which puts you outside PSA 10 territory and likely in PSA 9 range at best.

Two practical notes:

- **Vintage cards (Base Set through EX era)** often have centering tolerances that look worse than they grade, because PSA accounts for production-era printing variation. Don't auto-discard a Base Set Charizard over 65/35 centering without doing more research.
- **Modern cards (Sword and Shield onward)** are held to tighter standards because Pokémon Company International improved manufacturing. A 2026 Destined Rivals SIR at 63/37 is almost certainly a PSA 9 ceiling.

## Surfaces: What a Loupe Actually Shows You

Surface defects are the most common reason a card that looks clean to the naked eye comes back a PSA 9 instead of a 10. You need a 10x loupe or a USB microscope. Phone cameras work in a pinch but miss fine print lines and micro-scratches.

**What to look for under magnification:**

- **Print lines:** thin parallel lines running across the card face, usually horizontal, from the printing drum. They're common on Evolving Skies and Lost Origin cards. A single prominent print line typically costs you a grade point.
- **Scratches on the holo layer:** visible as fine directional lines on foil cards. Tilt the card at 45 degrees under a direct light source (a desk lamp, not overhead lighting). Scratches that are invisible face-on become obvious at an angle.
- **Silvering on black borders:** black-bordered cards (certain promos, Japanese sets) show silver wear at the edges of the card face. Even one silver fleck on a black border is a PSA 9 at best.
- **Staining or fingerprints:** oil from handling transfers to the card surface and shows under UV light. A $15 UV flashlight is one of the highest-ROI purchases for any submission pipeline.

<Callout variant="warning">Never clean a card with any liquid or cloth before grading. PSA graders can detect cleaning, and a cleaned card may receive a "C" qualifier that tanks resale value permanently. If a surface defect requires cleaning, that card is raw inventory, not a grading candidate.</Callout>

## Edges: The Forgotten Grade Killer

Collectors spend a lot of time on surfaces and not enough on edges. Run your fingernail very lightly along all four edges. You're feeling for chips, dings, or rough texture. Then examine each edge under your loupe at an angle.

Edge chipping is especially common on cards that were:
- Stored loose in a box or tin without sleeves
- Pulled from packs and immediately handled by multiple people
- Shipped raw in a plain envelope

A single white chip on a dark card border is a PSA 8 or lower. On lighter-bordered modern cards, edge chips are harder to see without magnification, which is exactly why they get missed in amateur pre-grades.

**The edge test for PSA 10 candidates:** under a direct light source, rotate the card slowly while holding it at eye level. The edge should look uniformly flat with no white intrusions. Any bright white spot that isn't part of the card's intended design is a chip.

## Corners: Four Points, Zero Tolerance

PSA 10 requires all four corners to show zero fraying under a loupe. Even slight fraying, which PSA describes as microscopic separation of the card layers at a corner point, drops a card to PSA 9.

The practical test: hold the card at a low angle under direct light and examine each corner. Then flip the card and check the back corners independently. Back corners are frequently worse than front corners because collectors tend to handle the back carelessly.

**Corner softness vs. fraying:** these are different defects. A soft corner has lost its sharp point without visible fiber separation; it grades PSA 9 or sometimes PSA 8 depending on severity. Fraying shows visible white fibers at the corner tip and typically grades PSA 9 or lower. Both disqualify a card from PSA 10.

## Building a Simple Pre-Grade Scoring Sheet

Before scanning or logging a card, run it through this checklist. Any single "fail" means the card is unlikely to grade PSA 10; two or more fails usually means PSA 9 or below.

| Criterion | PSA 10 Threshold | Your Card | Pass/Fail |
|---|---|---|---|
| Front centering (L/R) | 55/45 or better | ___/___ | |
| Front centering (T/B) | 55/45 or better | ___/___ | |
| Back centering (L/R) | 55/45 or better | ___/___ | |
| Surface (front, loupe) | No scratches, print lines | | |
| Surface (back, loupe) | No scratches, staining | | |
| Edges (all four) | No chips, no roughness | | |
| Corners (all four, front) | No fraying, no softness | | |
| Corners (all four, back) | No fraying, no softness | | |

Cards that pass all eight rows are your PSA 10 candidates. Cards that fail one row, typically centering or a single minor corner, are PSA 9 candidates worth submitting if the card's PSA 9 value justifies the $25 or more fee. Cards that fail two or more rows stay raw.

## Which Cards Actually Justify the Fee in 2026

Not every PSA 10 is worth chasing. The fee math only works when the PSA 10 premium over a strong raw price is meaningful. Here's a rough framework using 2026 market conditions:

- **Cards where PSA 10 is worth pursuing:** any raw card trading above $80 where the PSA 10 comparable is $200 or more. The Moonbreon PSA 10 market has hovered well above $500 in early 2026; a raw PSA 10-candidate copy at $250-300 makes the submission math work.
- **Cards where PSA 9 is the realistic ceiling and still worth it:** cards with raw values between $40 and $80 where PSA 9 adds $30-50 in documented resale lift. Check eBay completed sales for the specific card, filtered to graded sold listings, before committing.
- **Cards where neither grade justifies the fee:** cards trading raw below $30. At $25 per card in fees plus shipping overhead, you need a significant grade premium to break even. Most sub-$30 raw cards don't have it.

## One Mistake That Costs Submissions Every Time

The single most common pre-grade error: identifying a card by artwork instead of by name plus set code plus collector number. Two cards can share an identical illustration at different collector numbers and grade completely differently due to print run variation. A Charizard ex Special Illustration Rare from Obsidian Flames (set code OBF, collector number 234) is a different submission from a superficially similar card from a different set, with different population reports and different comps.

Always confirm name, set code, and collector number before pricing the submission and before pulling comps. This is the failure mode that inflates people's expected grade value. If you're pricing the wrong variant, your entire submission calculus is off.
