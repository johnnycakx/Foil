A PSA 9 and a PSA 8 on the same Moonbreon can mean the difference between a $400 sale and a $180 sale, and you can filter most PSA 8s out before you ever print a shipping label.

This is a working pre-grade checklist built from the same criteria PSA graders apply, organized so you can run it at your desk in under five minutes per card. Before anything else, read the [pokemon card condition guide](/pokemon-card-condition-guide) to calibrate your eye, then come back here for the submission-specific layer.

## Why Pre-Grading Pays for Itself

PSA's 2026 standard service sits at $30 per card (for cards valued under $499), and the economy tier has been discontinued. At that price, sending a card that grades PSA 7 costs you $30 in fees plus the slab locks your card into a lower-value tier. A raw Charizard ex SIR in ungraded NM/M condition sells around $90 on TCGplayer as of June 2026; the same card in a PSA 7 holder moves for roughly $110, a $20 premium that barely covers the fee after eBay's cut. A PSA 10, by contrast, has sold in the $340 range. The math only works if you're submitting cards with a realistic shot at 9 or 10.

The top Reddit threads on this topic (r/PokemonTCG, June 2026) give solid general advice but don't walk through a repeatable inspection sequence. PokéFolio's pre-grade guide filters on centering and surface but skips the three-field identification problem that causes rejected submissions. This post covers both.

PSA rejects cards it cannot authenticate. Before you inspect condition, confirm you can read all three fields off the card: name, set code, and collector number. Those three fields are what PSA's intake process uses to verify the card is legitimate. A blurry or missing set code on a reprint is a red flag worth resolving before submission.

## Step 1: Identify the Card with Three Fields, Not Artwork

This sounds obvious but costs people real money. Identifying a card by artwork alone leads to wrong-set submissions. The correct sequence: read the **name**, the **set code** (bottom-left on most modern cards, e.g., "SVI", "OBF", "PRE"), and the **collector number** (e.g., 201/167). These three fields determine which print run PSA is grading and which population report entry your slab lands in.

Pop differences matter. As of mid-2026, the Garchomp ex SIR (Obsidian Flames, 231/197) has a meaningfully different PSA 10 pop than the standard art version, and the market prices diverge by over $150 at the top grade. Mixing them up at submission wastes a $30 fee on the wrong card.

Foil's scan data, across cards processed through the app, consistently shows that set code misreads are the single most common identification error collectors make before submission. The name field is easy; the set code and collector number are where people guess instead of read. Don't guess.

## Step 2: Centering, The Easiest Filter

PSA grades centering on both front and back. The front threshold CenterGrade's published data confirms: **55/45 or better for a PSA 10**, **60/40 or better for a PSA 9**. Anything worse than 60/40 on the front is almost certainly an 8 or below, and you should pull it from the submission.

How to measure without a micrometer: lay the card on a flat white surface under consistent lighting. Use the card's own printed border as the reference. If the white border on one side is visibly more than half-again as wide as the opposite border, you're likely outside 60/40. A cheap transparent ruler gives you exact millimeter readings in 30 seconds.

Back centering is often ignored. PSA grades it separately. A card with perfect front centering and a badly off-center back can still lose points. Check both.

<Callout variant="tip">
For cards with full-art or alt-art prints (Moonbreon, Rayquaza V Alt, Garchomp ex SIR), centering visually reads differently because there's no white border on the front to reference. Measure the foil edge-to-frame distance instead, and compare it against a known PSA 10 image in the population report.
</Callout>

## Step 3: Corners, What a PSA 10 Corner Actually Looks Like

PSA 10 requires corners that show no wear under 10x magnification. In practice, hold the card at roughly a 30-degree angle under a single-point light source (a phone flashlight works). Four corners, four inspections.

What you're looking for:

| Corner condition | Likely PSA grade ceiling |
|---|---|
| Perfectly sharp, no fraying under 10x loupe | 9 or 10 |
| Slight surface wear visible only at angle | 8 |
| Visible whitening under normal light | 7 or below |
| Peeling or bent corner | 5 or below |

Pull any card with whitening visible under normal room light. It's not a PSA 9. That's a hard filter that removes a significant share of raw near-mint cards from submission consideration, which is the right outcome.

## Step 4: Surfaces, Front and Back Separately

Surface damage includes scratches, print lines, and holo pattern disturbance. The front holo surface on modern SIR and alt-art cards is especially susceptible to fine scratches from sleeve friction.

Inspection method: hold the card face-up and tilt it slowly toward the light. Fine scratches show as bright lines crossing the holo pattern. One or two micro-scratches in an unobtrusive area typically lands at PSA 9. Scratches crossing the main artwork are PSA 8 or lower.

Check the non-holo back with the same tilting technique. Print lines on the back (light parallel streaks from the press) are a manufacturing defect, not collector damage, and PSA treats them differently from surface scratches. Mark them but don't auto-pull the card.

<Callout variant="info">
Cards from certain 2025 and 2026 sets, including several White Flare and Mega Evolution prints, have shown elevated rates of factory print lines on the back. If you're submitting cards from those sets, inspect backs under magnification before assuming they're clean.
</Callout>

## Step 5: Edges, The Detail Everyone Rushes

Edge chipping is easy to miss on dark-bordered cards. The Evolving Skies era and Crown Zenith cards with black or dark frames disguise edge wear that's immediately visible on white-bordered Base Set cards.

Method: hold the card vertically between two fingers and rotate it slowly under the light, inspecting all four edges in sequence. Chipping shows as small bright spots on a dark border or as slight indentations on a white border.

A single small chip on a corner-adjacent edge typically caps at PSA 9. Multiple chips on any edge: PSA 8 or below, pull it.

## Step 6: Build a Submission Threshold

After running steps 1-5, you should have three piles:

- **Submit:** passes all five checks, realistic shot at PSA 9 or 10
- **Hold:** borderline on one criterion, research comparable sold slabs first
- **Skip:** obvious flaw, raw sale outperforms a graded PSA 7 after fees

For the "Hold" pile, check eBay completed sales filtered to PSA 8 for that specific card. If a PSA 8 of the card you're holding sells for $55 and raw NM sells for $40, the $30 fee produces a $15 gain before shipping. That's not worth the risk. If the PSA 8 sells for $150 and raw sells for $90, the math is better, though still thin.

PokeBeard noted in a recent video that he applies a simple ROI filter: if the PSA 10 price isn't at least 3x the raw price, the submission economics rarely justify the risk at standard service rates. That's speaker-data, not financial advice, but the ratio aligns with what the fee structure actually demands.

## Step 7: Pre-Grade Documentation Before You Pack

Before any card goes into a submission bag, photograph it:

- Front under flat lighting
- Back under flat lighting
- All four corners at 45-degree angle
- Both edges under raking light

This documentation serves two purposes. First, it gives you a pre-ship condition record if a card comes back damaged in transit or with a grade that doesn't match your inspection. Second, it forces you to slow down and actually look at each card rather than batch-stuffing sleeves.

For high-value submissions, above roughly $200 raw value per card, add a 10x loupe inspection to the documentation step. The difference between a PSA 10 and PSA 9 on a $200 raw card can be $300 or more in slab value, which makes five extra minutes of inspection highly productive.

## What to Do With the Cards You Don't Submit

Raw cards that don't clear your pre-grade threshold aren't worthless. A PSA 7 candidate in raw condition can still sell for $25-$60 on TCGplayer depending on the card, and raw buyers understand they're buying condition risk. List with an honest condition note ("edge chip, front corner wear, photos attached") and price it below NM market.

For cards where the raw value is under $15, grading rarely makes sense at current fee levels regardless of grade ceiling. That's a firm floor for 2026 submission economics, not a soft preference.

You can also check pricing before deciding whether a card is worth submitting at all using the [Foil card price lookup](/), which pulls eBay sold, TCGplayer market, and PriceCharting graded ladder data for any card identified by name, set code, and collector number. The graded ladder view shows the PSA 9 and PSA 10 sold prices alongside the raw price, which makes the submission ROI calculation immediate rather than requiring separate tab-hopping.

The pre-grade process isn't glamorous. It's a rejection protocol. Its job is to protect your $30 per card from going toward slabs that will sit in your collection at a loss. Run it every time, including on cards you've owned for years, because storage wear happens and your memory of a card's condition at purchase isn't the same as its condition today.
