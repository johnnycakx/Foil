A raw Garchomp ex SIR from sv3a Raging Surf sells for somewhere between $40 and $70 on TCGplayer right now, depending on the week, but the PSA 10 version of that same card tells a completely different story.

Sv3a Raging Surf released in Japan on September 22, 2023, and it remains one of the tighter Scarlet and Violet era chase sets. The card pool is small, the Super Illustration Rares are genuinely scarce, and unlike some S-era sets that flooded the secondary market, Raging Surf never got a domestic English reprint with the same pull rates. If you want the full picture on [japanese pokemon cards value](/japanese-pokemon-cards-value), this set is a useful case study in how a compact Japanese set ages differently from a mass-market English release.

This guide covers every card worth tracking, ranked by current secondary market price, with graded ladder data and sourcing notes.

## The Two Cards That Define the Set's Floor and Ceiling

Raging Surf has a clear hierarchy. Two cards do the heavy lifting on set value: **Garchomp ex SIR (081/071)** and **Palafin ex SIR (079/071)**. Everything else is supporting cast.

Garchomp ex SIR is the set's anchor. As of mid-2026, raw copies on TCGplayer market are trading in the $45-$65 range. PSA 10 submissions have cleared $130-$160 at auction, though pop has climbed since early 2025 and the spread between raw and PSA 10 has compressed slightly. PokeBeard flagged Garchomp ex SIR as a card worth monitoring in his recent content, but attributed the price firmness to genuine collector demand rather than speculator activity, which is a meaningful distinction when evaluating whether to buy raw or submit.

Palafin ex SIR (080/071) is the sleeper. It draws less YouTube oxygen than Garchomp but has held its raw price in the $30-$50 range through 2025 and into 2026 without significant bleed. PSA 10 copies have sold in the $90-$120 band (approximate). The card has a cleaner centering profile than Garchomp ex SIR on average, which matters when you're deciding whether a raw copy is worth the $25-$35 PSA grading fee.

<Callout variant="tip">
When identifying either SIR for pricing, read the collector number directly off the card: Garchomp ex SIR is **081/071**, Palafin ex SIR is **080/071**. The over-number format (081 out of a stated set total of 071) is the quick confirm that you're holding a Special Art card, not a standard ex. Identifying by artwork alone is how misidentification happens.
</Callout>

## The Graded Ladder: When Does It Make Sense to Submit?

The PSA grading fee structure as of 2026 makes the math on sv3a submissions fairly clean. At the standard tier (approximately $25 per card at current PSA pricing), the breakeven question is whether a PSA 10 is likely to return at least $25 over the raw price after fees and shipping.

For Garchomp ex SIR, the answer is yes, assuming you can source a centering-clean raw copy below $50. The PSA 10 premium on this card has historically run $80-$100 over a solid raw, which is enough margin to justify submission even accounting for the grading fee and the possibility of a PSA 9 outcome.

For the Supporting Full Arts (Cynthia, Arven), the math is marginal. A raw Cynthia Full Art at $13 submitted for $25 needs to PSA 10 at $55+ to clear costs. PSA 10 pops on these trainer Full Arts are thinner than the ex SIRs, which helps the premium, but centering on Japanese trainer Full Arts from sv3a has been inconsistent. Inspect the card under direct light before committing to a grading submission.

## What the Booster Box Market Says About Single Prices

Sealed sv3a Raging Surf booster boxes are currently listed on TCGplayer as low as $70, with some sellers asking $115 or more depending on condition and provenance. That range matters for singles pricing because it sets the expected value ceiling on pulling a chase card.

At a $70 box price and approximately 10 packs per box, the expected value math only works if you're targeting PSA 10 submissions on SIR pulls. Buying singles outright is almost always the correct move for collectors who want specific cards, not sealed product. A raw Garchomp ex SIR at $55 is a more predictable acquisition than gambling on a box that statistically gives you a small fraction of a chance at an SIR pull.

PikaPikaPapa has made similar sealed-vs-singles arguments about other sv-era sets in his recent content, noting that boxes trading below their MSRP equivalent create strange EV dynamics. The logic applies directly to sv3a: if a sealed box is available below $80, the sealed product itself may carry more upside than the singles it contains, purely because the floor on sealed is stickier than the floor on raw singles once supply dries up.

## Collector Numbers: Why They Matter for sv3a Specifically

Sv3a is a 71-card base set with 10 over-number cards (072/071 through 081/071). Every chase card lives in that over-number range. If you're scanning a card to price it, reading the collector number in the bottom left corner is the definitive method: anything 072 through 081 with a slash-071 denominator is in the premium tier. Anything below 072/071 is a standard rare or common.

This is exactly the identification failure mode that causes pricing errors on reseller platforms. A Garchomp ex standard rare (060/071) and a Garchomp ex SIR (081/071) feature the same Pokémon, but the price difference is roughly $45. Reading name plus set code plus collector number: sv3a, 081/071, Garchomp ex, is the three-field check that removes the ambiguity entirely.

Foil's scan data covers this set, and the name-plus-number read is precisely what the scanner resolves automatically rather than relying on artwork recognition, which fails on alternate art variants that share the same illustrated character.

## Grade Sensitivity: The PSA 9 vs PSA 10 Spread

For sv3a specifically, the PSA 9 to PSA 10 spread is steep on the SIRs. A PSA 9 Garchomp ex SIR trades in the $55-$75 range, which overlaps with a strong raw copy. A PSA 10 clears $130+. That means a PSA 9 result essentially returns you close to raw value after fees, while a PSA 10 generates a real premium.

This is not a set where PSA 9s hold collector interest well. The population of PSA 9s has grown consistently since 2025, while the PSA 10 pop remains comparatively tight. If you're submitting sv3a SIRs, you're playing for a 10 or you're better off staying raw.

The Cynthia Full Art (074/071) is the exception: PSA 9 copies have sold in the $28-$38 range, which can still clear costs on a standard-tier submission if the raw was sourced cheaply. Trainer Full Arts from this set tend to grade more consistently than the ex SIRs because the print quality on trainers was slightly cleaner.
