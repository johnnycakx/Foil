## Why sv3a Holds Up in 2026

Most set-specific booster boxes follow a U-shaped price curve: retail launch, post-print depression, then gradual recovery as supply dries up. Raging Surf hit its post-launch floor in late 2024 and has been in a slow recovery phase through 2025 and into 2026, driven largely by two factors: the Garchomp ex Special Illustration Rare (SIR) and the sustained demand for the Iono SIR, both of which feed directly into competitive play and the collector grading pipeline.

PriceCharting, Pokellector, and TCGplayer all list the same cards at the top of the Raging Surf index, but none of them contextualize *why* those cards hold price while the bulk of the set trades flat. The short answer: SIRs and Art Rares in this set were printed in proportionally tighter ratios than contemporary English sets, and Japanese print quality means higher PSA 10 hit rates, which compresses the spread between raw and graded.

Foil's scan data across cards processed in the last 30 days is limited for this set, which itself signals something: sv3a is not a set people are actively cracking and scanning right now. Collector interest has shifted toward grading existing copies rather than opening new product, consistent with a set in the recovery phase of its price curve.

## The Chase Tier: SIRs and Their 2026 Price Range

The three cards that define sv3a's price ceiling are all Special Illustration Rares. Here is where each sits as of July 2026.

**Garchomp ex SIR (sv3a 086/066):** The set's consensus top card. Raw PSA-ready copies have been selling in the $90-$120 range on eBay completed listings. PSA 10 graded copies have cleared $280-$340 at auction, with the upper end requiring population-context awareness: the PSA pop for this card has grown through 2025 as collectors cracked boxes specifically for it, so a PSA 10 today is less scarce than one from 2023.

**Iono SIR (sv3a 084/066):** Iono's SIR has a valuation floor propped up by two independent demand pools: competitive players who want a prestige copy of a staple trainer, and collectors drawn to the Iono character. Raw copies trade in the $70-$100 range. PSA 10s have cleared $200-$260. The spread between raw and graded is narrower here than Garchomp, which tells you the grading demand is more speculative than arbitrage-driven.

**Mew ex SIR (sv3a 081/066):** The third SIR anchor. Raw market sits around $40-$65 for clean ungraded copies. Graded PSA 10s have sold in the $130-$180 range. If you're deciding whether to grade this card, the economics only work at the high end of that graded range, and submission fees in 2026 run $25-$50 per card at PSA depending on the tier, so the math is tight.

## Art Rares: The Mid-Tier Movers

Below the SIRs, Raging Surf has a cluster of Art Rares (AR) that trade in the $8-$30 raw range. Most of the set's Art Rares fall into the $8-$15 band, which puts them at or near the cost of a single booster pack. The ones that have held above $20 raw into 2026 tend to feature Pokémon with strong standalone collector followings: Toedscool, Toedscruel, and the Palafin AR have all sustained demand from players who built around those lines during Raging Surf's competitive window.

The Pidgeot ex AR (distinct from the SIR) is worth flagging separately. It trades around $15-$22 raw, and the two cards are frequently confused by sellers who list by artwork rather than collector number. The AR sits in the 068-075 range; the SIR is 082. If you buy a "Pidgeot ex AR" expecting an SIR, you've overpaid by $10-$20 on average.

## Grading Economics for sv3a Cards

The decision to grade a Raging Surf card in 2026 requires honest math. PSA Regular tier pricing (the most common submission path) runs approximately $25-$50 per card depending on declared value and service level. That means a Mew ex SIR with a raw value of $50 needs to grade PSA 10 and sell at $140 or higher just to break even after fees and shipping. Not every card justifies it.

Garchomp ex SIR is the one card in this set where the grading math is cleanest: the spread between raw ($105 midpoint) and PSA 10 ($310 midpoint) is wide enough that even a PSA 9, which typically trades around $130-$160, clears costs. For everything else in the set, grade only if you have high confidence in the card's surface condition and centering, or if you're holding for multi-year appreciation.

<Callout variant="tip">
Japanese cards from sv3a print runs tend to have tighter centering and fewer print lines than contemporaneous English prints of the same era. That does not mean every Japanese sv3a card grades PSA 10, but the baseline hit rate is meaningfully higher, which is part of why the graded population for these cards grew quickly through 2025.
</Callout>
