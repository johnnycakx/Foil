**Subject:** eBay sold vs TCGplayer market: which number wins
**Preview text:** The price gap that's costing sellers real money

<p>A Mega Charizard X showing a gap of over $100 between its TCGplayer market price and its trailing eBay sold average in the same window. Same card, same condition, same week. That's not a glitch. It's a structural difference in who buys where, and if you're pricing off only one source, you're working with half the picture.</p>

<p>The new post unpacks exactly why those two numbers diverge, and more importantly, when each one is the right one to use.</p>

<h2>The short version</h2>

<p>TCGplayer market price is a rolling weighted average of completed sales on TCGplayer only. No eBay transactions, no buylist sales, no Discord trades. For competitive staples under $50 with deep listing volume, it's reliable and fast to use. For anything above roughly $150, the buyer pool on TCGplayer thins out, volume drops, and the price can lag eBay sold by one to two weeks during a run-up.</p>

<p>eBay sold covers a broader buyer base and moves faster. When a card spikes, eBay sold reflects it within hours. TCGplayer market price may not catch up for days. The tradeoff: eBay's data includes Best Offer acceptances that compress the average below what you'd expect from listed prices, and eBay's fee structure as of 2026 runs higher than TCGplayer's, so gross sold prices on eBay tend to sit a few dollars above TCGplayer on identical raw cards. That gap is a fee artifact, not a valuation signal.</p>

<h2>Three spots where the gap is biggest</h2>

<p><strong>High-cap raw cards above $150.</strong> Buyers at that price shop carefully and lean on eBay for purchase protection. TCGplayer's volume is thin at the high end, so its market price calculation has a small denominator and lags behind real transactions.</p>

<p><strong>Low-inventory sets.</strong> If a set hasn't been cracked heavily by TCGplayer sellers, two sales at very different prices can produce a market average that reflects neither. eBay, with more comps in the same window, gives you something to work with.</p>

<p><strong>Graded slabs.</strong> TCGplayer's slab market exists but is thin. For PSA 10 copies of any meaningful card, eBay sold is the only source with enough volume to price from. Use it exclusively for slabs.</p>

<h2>Where TCGplayer wins</h2>

<p>Raw cards under $50 with active listings: competitive staples, bulk rares, anything moving at volume through TCGplayer's buyer base. The market price is accurate, it's updated regularly, and it tells you exactly what you'll compete against when you list. For bulk pricing decisions across hundreds of cards, TCGplayer's database also does the identification work automatically, which eBay search doesn't.</p>

<h2>How to use both</h2>

<p>The spread between the two sources is itself a signal. A $180 TCGplayer market price sitting next to a $155 eBay sold average on the same raw card means sellers are holding firm on TCGplayer while buyers are transacting lower on eBay. That's a buyer's market on one platform and a price-cut opportunity on the other. Ignoring either number leaves you guessing at which reality is current.</p>

<p>For most cards above $30, checking both sources takes about 90 seconds. The post includes a decision table that maps each situation (listing a raw card, grading a slab, buying from a local seller, valuing a collection) to the right source. PriceCharting's graded price ladder also gets its due, particularly for deciding whether to submit a card for grading.</p>
