<p>A raw Charizard ex from Obsidian Flames sold for $47 on eBay last week. The same card in a PSA 10 slab moved for $210. If you don't know which version you're holding — or whether your card is even from that set — you're guessing, not valuing.</p>

<p>The blog post I just published walks through a five-step checklist that collapses the valuation process to 60 seconds. Here's what it covers and why each step matters.</p>

<h2>Three fields, not the artwork</h2>

<p>Identifying a card by how it looks is the single most common mispricing mistake I see. <strong>Name, set code, and collector number</strong> are the only data points that matter. "Pikachu" spans hundreds of distinct printings — prices range from $0.10 for a bulk common to over $600 for certain promos. Two cards that look nearly identical can differ by $300 once you read the set code and collector number. If your card's collector number exceeds the set total — say, 223/197 — you're holding a Secret Rare, and that single check can mean the difference between a $15 card and a $150 card.</p>

<h2>Condition moves the number before you open a browser</h2>

<p>TCGplayer's Near Mint market price is the default figure most tools display. But one whitened corner drops your card to Lightly Played — and LP typically trades at a <strong>15–25% discount to NM</strong>. On a card worth $30 or more raw, that's real cash. On a high-end card, the gap between a PSA 9 and a PSA 10 can exceed $100. Check centering under bright light at a 45-degree angle before you assume NM.</p>

<h2>Three sources, not one</h2>

<p>Single-source pricing is how you leave money on the table or overpay at a local game store. The stack I use on every card over $15: <strong>eBay sold listings (last 30 days)</strong> for real transaction data, <strong>TCGplayer market price</strong> for the platform rolling average, and <strong>PriceCharting's graded ladder</strong> when I'm weighing whether to submit to PSA.</p>

<p>Foil's scan data across cards processed in the last 30 days shows the spread between a card's TCGplayer market price and its 30-day eBay sold median averaged roughly 18%. Collectors relying on a single source were off by nearly a fifth of the card's actual market value.</p>

<h2>Grading math is simple — most people skip it</h2>

<p>PSA's Economy tier runs approximately $25 per card as of 2026. That fee needs to be cleared by the graded premium before submission makes sense. The math usually works at $80+ raw NM value, or for cards with a documented low PSA 10 pop count. Below that threshold, you're often paying $25 to find out your card grades a 8.</p>

<h2>Set age and reprint risk</h2>

<p>New sets trend down for the first 3–6 months as sealed product floods the secondary market. Out-of-print older sets trend up as supply depletes. If you're sitting on a card from a set released in the last six months, the price you see today may be 20–30% above where it stabilizes. Reprint announcements can cut a card's value to a third of its pre-announcement price — monitor official set reveals before committing to a sale price.</p>
