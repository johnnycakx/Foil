<p>A Japanese SAR of the same character typically sells for 15–40% more than its English SIR on eBay sold listings as of mid-2026. That gap is real. It's also card-dependent, grade-dependent, and shrinking on some titles — which means acting on the headline number without checking the specifics is how you overpay or undersell.</p>

<p>I wrote a full breakdown on the blog: <a href="/blog/japanese-sar-vs-english-special-illustration-rare">Read the full post →</a></p>

<h2>What the price spread actually looks like by grade</h2>

<p>The 20–35% JP premium shows up most clearly at PSA 10. On a Charizard ex, that's a ceiling of roughly <strong>$350–$480 for the Japanese SAR</strong> versus <strong>$280–$380 for the English SIR</strong>. On a Gardevoir ex, the spread is similar in structure but tighter in dollar terms. At PSA 9, the gap narrows to somewhere in the 10–20% range. Raw and ungraded, you're sometimes looking at only $5–$15 separating versions on mid-tier cards — which makes sourcing JP specifically to flip raw a thin proposition.</p>

<p>The JP premium is not universal. Three scenarios where English SIR matches or beats it:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Western-skewing demand.</strong> Certain Eeveelutions and Pikachu variants pull harder in English markets. Collector demand can close or flip the gap entirely.</li>
<li><strong>Grading cost errors.</strong> Misidentifying a JP collector number — a three-digit code in small text — gets your submission returned ungraded. English cards are easier to confirm before you ship anything.</li>
<li><strong>PSA 10 hit rate.</strong> On some prints, English cards grade PSA 10 at a higher rate because the slightly heavier stock handles edge damage better in transit. If the English hit rate is meaningfully higher, expected value can exceed JP even at a lower per-card ceiling.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The mistake I see constantly: pricing by artwork</h2>

<p>Two SARs with nearly identical art — different variant printings of the same trainer — can differ by more than $150 at PSA 10. The correct identification method is <strong>name + set code + collector number</strong>, read directly from the card. For Japanese cards, the collector number sits in the bottom right, formatted like 076/066. SAR numbers exceed the standard set count, which is the tell. Pricing by artwork alone skips this entirely.</p>

<p>The obvious arbitrage between JP and English versions is mostly gone — the market is more efficient than it was two years ago. The edge now lives in knowing the exact card identity, checking the current PSA population before you submit, and running the grade-level spread per card rather than assuming the JP version always wins.
