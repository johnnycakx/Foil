**Subject:** The NM/LP gap that costs you $180 on one card
**Preview text:** One condition grade, 38-45% less money — here's the line

---

<p>A single scuff on the wrong card can cost you $50. On a Base Set Charizard, the gap between Near Mint and Lightly Played is roughly $180 — more than the total value of most modern chase cards.</p>

<p>That's the number that should reframe how seriously you take the NM/LP boundary. Most sellers don't. Foil's scan data from the last 30 days shows the most common correction flagged is <strong>back-edge whitening that the seller hadn't noticed</strong>. Not scratches on the face. Not corner rounding. The back edge — the thing you skip when you're doing a face-forward inspection the way you'd display the card.</p>

<p>Flip that habit. Inspect the back first.</p>

<h2>What the line actually looks like</h2>

<p>NM means zero edge whitening, no visible scratches on the face at 45° under direct light, intact surface gloss, and corners that still come to a visible point. LP means minor whitening on one or two edges, light scratches catchable at that same 45° angle, and corners showing the earliest stage of rounding. <em>One rounded corner is enough to call LP.</em> One visible scratch on the face drops the card regardless of edge condition.</p>

<p>The discount for crossing that line clusters between 38–45% across the card values cited in the full post — whether you're talking about a ~$55 Pikachu VMAX or a ~$210 Umbreon VMAX Alt Art. That compression isn't random. It reflects what the competitive buyer pool knows: a true NM card is a viable PSA 9 submission candidate. An LP card closes that pathway, and buyers price accordingly.</p>

<h2>The grading math that should change how you buy</h2>

<p>A ~$120 Lugia V Alt Art in LP bought at ~$72, submitted for grading at $25–$35 in fees, typically returns a PSA 6 worth approximately $90. You've broken even at best. The same card in NM bought at $120 returns a PSA 9 in approximately the $280–$320 range. The math isn't close — and it starts entirely with whether the card crossed the NM/LP line before you bought it.</p>

<p>For cards under $25 or built for active play, LP is often the correct economic choice — same gameplay, 38–40% less cost. But for anything $50 and up where grading is even a possibility within two years, buying LP to save money now is usually the more expensive decision long-term.</p>

<h2>Three fields before you grade anything</h2>

<p>Before condition even matters, you need a correct identification: <strong>name, set code, collector number</strong>. Two cards can share artwork and differ by $300 because one is a holo and one isn't. Artwork recognition isn't identification. Those three fields off the card are.</p>

<p>Once you have the right card, <em>then</em> assess condition. Not before.</p>
