<p>A single white edge nick dropped a Charizard ex from $180 to $90. That's the NM/LP boundary in one transaction — and if you're grading by feel instead of by criteria, you're either leaving money on the table or setting up a dispute.</p>

<p>I published a deep post on this today because the guides that already exist define the categories fine but don't show you what the price gap actually looks like on current cards. That gap is the whole point.</p>

<h2>The gap is not flat</h2>

<p>On budget commons, NM versus LP is a few cents. Nobody files a dispute over a penny. On chase cards, the spread compounds fast. The blog documents three 2026 data points directly:</p>

<p><strong>Charizard ex (Obsidian Flames, 223/197):</strong> NM copies selling $175–$195. LP copies clearing $88–$105. Roughly a 50% haircut on one condition call.</p>

<p><strong>Rayquaza VMAX (Evolving Skies, 217/203):</strong> NM around $130, LP around $72. A $58 spread on a single card.</p>

<p><strong>Base Set Shadowless Charizard (raw):</strong> NM-condition raws at $900–$1,200+. A copy sitting at the LP threshold — visible corner wear — drops to $400–$550. The gap exceeds $400.</p>

<p>The pattern is consistent: the higher the NM price, the wider the absolute dollar gap at the NM/LP boundary. This is the most financially significant condition boundary in the entire spectrum, by a wide margin.</p>

<h2>Where sellers keep getting it wrong</h2>

<p>Two failure modes show up constantly. The first is over-grading — listing an LP card as NM, which ends in a dispute, a refund, or a negative. The second is under-grading, which is quieter but just as costly: collectors downgrading their own cards out of excessive caution and leaving 30–50% value behind on every listing.</p>

<p>The third error is foil-scratch confusion. Manufacturing lines on foil are explicitly excluded from grading criteria. A card with fine print lines from the factory is still NM. A card with a scratch that shows directionality or disrupts the foil pattern is LP. Those are not the same thing, and conflating them is how you misprice a card that should be in your NM column.</p>

<h2>The grading submission rule</h2>

<p>LP cards almost never justify grading fees. A PSA 6 or 7 adds some premium over raw LP value — but rarely enough to cover submission costs on sub-$100 raw cards. The post's rule of thumb: <strong>don't submit LP cards unless the raw LP value already exceeds roughly $150</strong>, where a PSA 7 or 8 result can meaningfully multiply the outcome. Grading submissions should be reserved for cards you've already confirmed are NM or better.</p>

<h2>The five-point checklist</h2>

<p>The full post walks through a practical checklist — edges, corners, face scratches, back surface, surface feel — that you run under natural light and under a 45-degree direct source. It takes under two minutes per card once you've internalized the criteria. If a card clears all five, it's NM. If it fails one, it's LP. Price it accordingly and say so in the listing. That's the fastest way to eliminate disputes and protect your seller reputation long-term.</p>
