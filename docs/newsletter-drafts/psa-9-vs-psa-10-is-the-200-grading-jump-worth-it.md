**Subject:** PSA 9 vs 10: when the premium is a trap
**Preview text:** The grading multiplier that flips by card era

---

<p>A PSA 9 Umbreon VMAX Alt Art is selling in the $175-$220 range. A PSA 10 runs $310-$380. That spread is real money, and the PSA 10 pop is several thousand copies. Whether you should care about that difference depends entirely on which side of that pop number you're standing on.</p>

<p>The blog post I just published works through the full framework: era-by-era multipliers, pop-report thresholds, submission math, and the three scenarios where a PSA 9 is the correct buy. Here's the short version of what I think actually matters.</p>

<h2>The multiplier is not flat</h2>

<p>For WOTC first-edition holos, the PSA 10 premium is punishing and justified. The pop on something like a Blastoise 1st Edition is often under 50 copies globally. You are not getting more of those. The scarcity is permanent. A 5x to 10x multiplier is rational when the supply of gradeable raw copies is finite and shrinking.</p>

<p>For modern SIRs and alt arts, the logic reverses. More product is moving every month. PokeBeach has been discussing Surging Sparks booster boxes at $235 each, which tells you the print run is still generating submissions. Every new PSA 10 that clears processing is another copy competing with yours. A 1.5x to 2x multiplier on a card with a five-figure pop is not the same animal as a 10x multiplier on a card with 40 PSA 10s in existence.</p>

<h2>The pop report is the variable most people skip</h2>

<p>The dollar spread between a 9 and a 10 is visible on any sales history chart. The pop number requires one extra click on PSA's site. That extra click is worth more than the chart. A PSA 10 pop over 1,000 means new copies enter the market every week. The premium exists, but it's thin and compressing. For those cards, a PSA 9 is almost always the better value buy.</p>

<p>The threshold I use: PSA 10 pop under 100 means the premium is durable. Over 1,000, I'm buying the 9 unless I have a specific reason to pay up.</p>

<h2>The submission math has a clear cutoff</h2>

<p>PSA's economy tier is $25 per card for cards valued under $499. The rule of thumb in the post holds up: if the PSA 10-to-9 dollar spread is less than 2x the submission fee, the grade chase probably isn't worth it. If it's more than 5x the submission fee, submit every near-mint copy you own. Everything in between requires you to actually run the expected value math on your realistic hit rate, not the optimistic one.</p>

<h2>Confirm the card identity before any of this matters</h2>

<p>The most common pre-submission error I see isn't misgrading a condition. It's misidentifying the card entirely: a promo reprint in a first-edition sleeve, a set-code mismatch on a promotional copy. Condition grades are worthless if the card identity is wrong. Foil reads the name, set code, and collector number from a photo so you know exactly what you're holding before you make any submission or purchase decision.</p>
